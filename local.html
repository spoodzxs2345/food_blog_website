Local Foods

It is not just more fun but also more food in the Philippines. Take a look at some of the all-time favorites.

Sinigang

Who would even forget this iconic local dish? It is even named as the best soup in the world by Taste Atlas during the international food database's annual 2021 Awards. This is also one of the easiest and quickest to make dish. This soup is characterized by its sour and savory taste. It is commonly associated with tamarind, but, as time went by, there have been different alternatives such as green mango, kamias, and many more. This dish can be served anytime at any place.

Origin

The reason why it is called “sinigang” is that it was a translation to the English word, “stew” which is true because of its method of cooking.

Sinigang is one of the country’s oldest dishes as it has indigenous origins and existed in the Philippines long before any colonizers set foot in the country.

Ingredients

  • 4¼ cups water
  • 2 cups pork spare rib cut into chunks
  • 1 pc red onion, quartered
  • 2 pcs medium sized tomato, quartered
  • 8 pcs sitaw, sliced into 2” pieces
  • 250 g kangkong stalk and leaves
  • 1 pc talong, sliced
  • 2 pcs siling pangsigang
  • sinigang mix or any sour agent
  • Optional: labanos, okra

How to cook?

  • Step 1
    • Begin by washing pork spare ribs in cold running water to remove any visible impurities. Then, place pork ribs submerge in a medium pot with a liter of water. Bring to a boil without covering the pot and allow the scum to float to the surface. Meticulously remove the scums from the surface of the water using a slotted spoon or a spatula. Scums are impurities found in meat.

  • Step 2
    • After the broth clears up, turn you heat to medium setting, then it’s time toss in your onions and tomatoes to add flavor to the broth. Cover your pot and let it simmer to fully cook your meat. Use a fork to pierce the meat so that the flavors would also enter the meaty parts of the spare ribs, You also do this so you can tell if it’s already set and tender.

  • Step 3
    • You can already add your vegetables. Start by adding the sitaw, kangkong stalks and talong into the pot and cook until these turn dark green. If you decide to use labanos and okra, you can also add those at this point. This should take roughly around 2-3 minutes.

  • Step 4
    • Add in your siling sigang, kangkong leaves and the sour agent. Stir and let it simmer. It should be done in about 2-3 minutes.

  • Step 5
    • All done! Transfer your Sinigang to a serving bowl. This is a happy treat that warms, soothes and satisfies everyone in the family! Remember to prepare extra servings of rice to enjoy the extra asim kilig taste of this Filipino classic soup. Enjoy!

Nutrients

This dish is not only a hearty and home-y soup often regarded as a quintessential Filipino comfort food, but can also help in boosting your immune system with Vitamin C from the tamarind base, treating constipation (since it is fiber rich), energy boosting from the protein, and packed with antioxidants and anti-inflammatory agents from the ginger.

Sisig

Undeniably one of the G.O.A.T. of Filipiono dishes. It is a dish that can be found anywhere in the country. It's like almost every region in the Philippines have their own version of sisig. This dish is typically served on a sizzling hot-plate that contains parts of a pig’s ear, snout, cheeks, and belly. It’s sauteed with onions, vinegar, garlic, and chicken liver.

Origin

This dish was originally a vegan food. According to Food Insider, Sisig was originally a vegan dish. It was a salad that included vegetables and fruits mixed with vinegar paste that was meant to be very sour.

There are many theories as to how the pork sisig originated but the most popular notion was during the American occupation of the Philippines during the late 1800s.

American Naval Forces would throw out left over pig parts that they wouldn’t use for cooking. Parts like the pig head, which Filipinos didn’t want to waste.

Initially sisig was just boiled pig parts soaked in vinegar. However, a barbeque owner, Aling Lucing, revolutionized the dish during the late 1960s, giving birth to the sisig we know and love today.

Ingredients

  • 1 lb. pig ears
  • 1 1/2 lb pork belly
  • 1 piece onion minced
  • 3 tablespoons soy sauce
  • 1 knob ginger minced (optional)
  • 1/2 teaspoon garlic powder
  • 3 to 5 pieces calamansi
  • ½ cup butter or margarine
  • ¼ lb chicken liver
  • 6 cups water
  • 1/2 teaspoon salt
  • 1 piece egg (optional)

How to cook?

  • Step 1
    • Pour the water in a pan and bring to a boil Add salt and pepper.

  • Step 2
    • Put-in the pig’s ears and pork belly then simmer for 40 minutes to 1 hour (or until tender).

  • Step 3
    • Remove the boiled ingredients from the pot then drain excess water.

  • Step 4
    • Grill the boiled pig ears and pork belly until done.

  • Step 5
    • Chop the pig ears and pork belly into fine pieces.

  • Step 6
    • In a wide pan, melt the butter or margarine. Add the onions. Cook until onions are soft.

  • Step 7
    • Put-in the ginger and cook for 2 minutes.

  • Step 8
    • Add the chicken liver. Crush the chicken liver while cooking it in the pan.

  • Step 9
    • Add the chopped pig ears and pork belly. Cook for 10 to 12 minutes.

  • Step 10
    • Put-in the soy sauce, garlic powder, and chili. Mix well.

  • Step 11
    • Add salt and pepper to taste.

  • Step 12
    • Put-in the mayonnaise and mix with the other ingredients.

  • Step 13
    • Transfer to a serving plate. Top with chopped green onions and raw egg.

  • Step 14
    • Serve hot. Share and Enjoy (add the lemon or calamansi before eating).

Nutrients

1 serving of pork sisig contains 2g total carbs, 2g net carbs, 5g fat, 8g protein, and 90 calories.

Ginataang Kalabasa

Let's even out the list and lower our calories with some delicious vegetable meal, shall we? Ginataang Kalabasa, also known as Ginataang Gulay to some, is healthy vegetable dish composed of yard-long beans, calabaza squash, shrimp, and thin slices of pork. The ingreddients are cooked in coconut milk and served with white rice.

Origin

Ginataang Gulay is just one of the many variations of ginataan along with ginataang alimango, ginataang hipon, adobo sa gata, and many more.

Ingredients

  • 1 lb. shrimp, peeled and deveined
  • 1/2 lb sitaw (green beans), cut into 2" length
  • 2 eggplants, cut into 1/2” thick rounds
  • 2 ampalaya, deseeded and cut into ½” thick strips
  • 4 cups squash, cubed
  • 6 pcs okra, sliced
  • 1/2 tbsp ginger, sliced thinly
  • 1 medium sized onion, sliced
  • 2 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 cup water
  • 1 ½ cups coconut milk
  • vegetable oil
  • 2 tbsp fish sauce
  • salt
  • pepper

How to cook?

  • Step 1
    • Wash and prepare the vegetables.

  • Step 2
    • Heat the oil. Saute the garlic, onion and ginger for about 5 minutes.

  • Step 3
    • Add the fish sauce. Let it simmer for a couple of minutes. Stir occasionally.

  • Step 4
    • Add the coconut milk and water. Continue to simmer until the mixture has reduced and thickened.

  • Step 5
    • Add the shrimp and stir constantly for 5 minutes. Add the squash and cook for about 2 minutes or until it becomes soft. Add the okra, eggplant, sitaw, and then the ampalaya at 2 minutes apart. Season with salt and pepper.

  • Step 6
    • Transfer to a serving dish and serve hot.

  • Step 7
    • Enjoy!

Nutrients

It is rich in vitamins such as vitamin A and C, and folate. Minerals such as manganese, iron, copper, calcium, and magnesium can be found in this vegetable. Pork and shrimp also provides nutrients.

Kwek-kwek

Local foods list is not complete without a street food. Kwek-kwek are boiled quail eggs and deep fried until the batter is crispy. It is one of the popular street foods in the Philippines along with fish ball, kikiam, calamares, squid balls, chicken balls, etc.

Origin

The kwek-kwek was an accidental variation on the balut snack. Legend has it that a balut vendor in Cubao once dropped her basket of eggs, cracking their shells and letting the precious, savory broth seep out.

Ingredients

  • 12 to 18 pieces boiled quail eggs
  • 1 cup flour
  • 3 tbsp cornstarch
  • 3/4 to 1 cup water
  • 1 tbsp anatto powder (pinulbos na atsuete)
  • 1/2 tsp salt
  • 1/2 tsp ground black pepper
  • 2 cups cooking oil

How to cook?

  • Step 1
    • Place the cornstarch in a container and dredge the boiled quail eggs. Set aside.

  • Step 2
    • In a mixing bowl, combine flour, salt, and pepper then mix thoroughly.

  • Step 3
    • Dilute the anatto powder in warm water then pour-in the mixing bowl with the other ingredients. Mix well.

  • Step 4
    • Place all the quail eggs in the mixing bowl and coat with the batter.

  • Step 5
    • Heat the pan and pour the cooking oil.

  • Step 6
    • When the oil is hot enough, deep-fry the quail eggs by scooping them from the mixing bowl using a spoon. Make sure that each is coated with batter.

  • Step 7
    • After a few minutes, remove the fried quail eggs from the pan and place in a serving plate.

  • Step 8
    • Serve with vinegar or fish ball sauce while still crispy.

  • Step 9
    • Share and Enjoy!

Nutrients

A piece of kwek-kwek contains 1g total carbs, 1g net carbs, 2g fat, 1g protein, and 20 calories.
